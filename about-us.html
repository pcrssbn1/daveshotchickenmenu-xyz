About Dave's Hot Chicken and This Menu Resource

The Dave's Hot Chicken Story and Rapid Expansion

Dave's Hot Chicken began in a parking lot in East Hollywood, Los Angeles, in early 2017. Chef Dave Kopushyan, along with friends Arman Oganesyan, Tommy Rubenyan, and brothers Amir and Gary Rubenyan, started serving Nashville-style hot chicken from a parking lot pop-up with no formal restaurant space. The concept was simple: perfectly fried chicken tenders and sliders with customizable heat levels, served fast and affordable. Lines formed immediately, with customers waiting up to two hours for their orders.

The parking lot operation caught the attention of investors and food enthusiasts throughout Los Angeles. By late 2017, they opened their first brick-and-mortar location in East Hollywood. The restaurant maintained the simple menu that made the pop-up successful while adding proper kitchen facilities and seating. Celebrity investors including Drake, Samuel L. Jackson, and Maria Sharapova joined the company in 2019, bringing both capital and publicity that accelerated expansion plans.

By 2024, Dave's Hot Chicken operates over 180 locations across the United States and Canada, with aggressive expansion plans targeting 1,000 locations by 2027. The company has maintained its core menu philosophy throughout this growth: focus on doing chicken exceptionally well rather than diluting the concept with extensive menu options. According to Nation's Restaurant News, Dave's Hot Chicken ranked among the fastest-growing restaurant chains in 2023 with a 156% unit growth rate. The success reflects broader consumer trends toward specialized fast-casual concepts that prioritize quality over variety.

The restaurant's expansion strategy focuses on franchise partnerships with experienced restaurant operators. Most locations maintain consistent menu offerings, though regional testing occurs in select markets. The brand has successfully entered competitive markets including Texas, Nevada, Illinois, and Florida while maintaining strong performance in its California home base. The company's growth trajectory mirrors other successful fast-casual chicken concepts but distinguishes itself through the customizable heat level system that creates a unique interactive dining experience.

Nashville Hot Chicken Tradition and Dave's Approach

Nashville hot chicken originated in the 1930s in Nashville, Tennessee, with Prince's Hot Chicken Shack credited as the originator. The story goes that Thornton Prince's girlfriend, upset with his philandering, served him extremely spicy fried chicken as revenge. Instead of being deterred, Prince loved the heat and developed it into a signature dish. The traditional preparation involves frying chicken pieces and coating them in a cayenne pepper paste mixed with oil, creating a distinctive red color and intense heat.

Dave's Hot Chicken adapts this Nashville tradition to a fast-casual format with several modifications. Traditional Nashville hot chicken uses bone-in chicken pieces (often quarters or halves), while Dave's focuses on boneless tenders and breast fillets for faster service and easier eating. The seven-tier spice system expands on Nashville's traditional mild-medium-hot-extra hot structure, providing more granular control for customers. This customization approach reflects California dining culture, where personalization is highly valued.

The restaurant maintains key elements of authentic Nashville hot chicken: the cayenne-based spice blend, the oil-based application method that makes the heat adhere to the chicken, and the traditional accompaniments of white bread and pickles. These accompaniments serve functional purposes—the bread absorbs excess oil and provides relief from heat, while pickles offer acidic contrast to the rich fried chicken. According to food historians at the Southern Foodways Alliance at the University of Mississippi, Nashville hot chicken experienced a national renaissance starting around 2010, with numerous restaurants adapting the style for different markets.

Dave's success demonstrates that regional food traditions can be successfully adapted and scaled nationally when core elements are respected while making strategic modifications for broader appeal. The fast-casual format, boneless chicken options, and expanded spice level system make Nashville hot chicken accessible to customers who might be intimidated by traditional preparations. This balance between authenticity and accessibility has proven commercially successful while introducing millions of customers to a distinctive Southern culinary tradition.
